MYSORE, August 9, 2011

Rituals at Mysore Dasara festival change with the times

  • Special Correspondent
print   ·   T  T  
Tweet

Rituals associated with Dasara in Mysore have evolved over the centuries. ‘Gajayapayana' is a case in point.

Reckoned to be a practice that started during the era of the Maharajas, the event was disbanded after the abolition of the Privy Purse and the monarchy. But it was revived in the recent years. However, the revised form has seen some changes. Nagendra, a priest who officiates in many rituals of the royal family, who was present at Veeranahosahalli during the event, explained that earlierthe Maharajas used to come to the jungles and personally offer prayers.

The Anjaneyaswami temple on the border of Nagarahole was frequented by the Maharajas, who would offer prayers for the welfare of the elephants. “Today, members of the royal family offer donations and we (priests) conduct the special pujas on behalf of the Wadiyars,” Mr. Nagendra added.

Also, the elephants used to traverse the entire distance from the jungle camps to the city on foot. But in the revised format, the elephants only walk a little distance at Veeranahosahalli, after which they are loaded onto lorries and brought to Mysore.

Though the elephants walked the entire distance for some years after the revival of Gajapayana, Forest Department officials and wildlife experts noticed that the practice of getting coconuts crushed by the elephants on the way resulted in severe bruises to some of the animals. Hence, the practice was stopped. In the days of the monarchy, it was the Maharaja who travelled on the elephants during the ‘Jumboo Savari' or royal procession on Vijayadashmi day. Now the elephants carry the golden howdah with an idol of the goddess Chamundeshwari on it.
